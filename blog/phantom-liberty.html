03. cyberpunk 2077 phantom liberty broke me

March 15, 2024 6 min read

i don't usually cry at games. i'm saying that not to prove anything about myself but just so you understand the weight of what i'm about to tell you. Phantom Liberty made me sit in silence after the credits for a genuinely uncomfortable amount of time.

the DLC gives you a choice. Reed or Song. and on the surface it looks like a tactical decision. who do you side with. who do you trust. what's the play. it is not a tactical decision.

if you go with Reed you get the ending where V might actually survive. there's a cure. there's a future. the catch is that So Mi — Song — gets handed back to the NUSA. gets put back in the box. after everything she went through to get out of it. you save yourself and you sell her back to the thing that broke her. and Reed lives with that. and you live with that. and the game doesn't let you forget it. the final conversation between V and Reed on that rooftop is one of the most quietly devastating things i've experienced in a game. two people who did something they can't take back, being honest with each other about it.

if you go with Song you get the ending where you lose. V doesn't get the cure. the relic keeps killing you. you have months maybe. but So Mi gets to be free. actually free. and there's something about choosing someone else's freedom over your own survival that the game renders so specifically it almost doesn't feel like fiction.

i chose Reed first. i needed to see it. and then i reloaded and chose Song. and that second playthrough hurt more because i already knew what i was giving up.

what Phantom Liberty understands that most games don't is that the most emotional choices aren't between good and evil. they're between two kinds of loss. you're not choosing who to save. you're choosing what you can live with. or in V's case — what you can die with.

i'm still thinking about it. clearly.
